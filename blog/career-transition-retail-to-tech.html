From retail to code: how I left 15 years of sales behind

Office boy, bakery, shopping mall, burger joint — and then, a terminal open and the feeling that this was it.

By Diego Torres · March 2026 · ⏱ 8 min read

I didn't plan to become a developer. I really didn't. For most of my adult life, I believed "programming was for math people," that "tech was for the young," and that I had found my place behind a counter, with sales targets and customers to attend to.

But there's one thing fifteen years in retail taught me: when you ignore what you truly want, the discomfort slowly grows until it becomes too loud to pretend you can't hear it.

"It wasn't a courageous decision. It was a necessary one — and the difference matters."

Where it all started: selling what others built

My career began as an office boy. Then an internship at a real estate agency. Then a bakery — I learned a lot about rhythm, about serving people in a hurry, about not getting the change wrong at 7am.

Then came the shopping mall years. Telecom sales, watch retail, HP, digital media. And finally, five years at iPlace selling Apple products — followed by two years training other salespeople in tech. That's where everything shifted.

What the iPlace years changed in my head

Spending five years selling Apple isn't simple. Customers arrive knowing what they want — or thinking they do. You need to understand hardware, software, device ecosystems. I started studying technology out of necessity and discovered I loved it.

As a training analyst, I spent two years teaching other salespeople to understand tech. And that's where I realized: I was more interested in the product than in selling it. I wanted to understand how things worked on the inside.

"I wanted to build, not just sell what others had built."

The burger joint and what failure teaches

Before going all-in on tech, I tried entrepreneurship. A burger restaurant. Almost a year of operations — one of the hardest and richest experiences I've ever had. I learned about margins, payroll, and how difficult it is to depend on so many variables at once.

It didn't work out the way I hoped. And when I closed it, I didn't feel defeat — I felt clarity. It was time to bet on what I'd really wanted since first getting into tech at iPlace.

Why tech? Why development?

I could have chosen cybersecurity. Infrastructure. I've studied both a bit. But web development grabbed me differently — the feeling of creating something from scratch, watching an interface come to life, solving a real problem with code.

💡 What retail gave me that a degree couldn't: the ability to understand the user before writing a single line. Fifteen years listening to customers made me a developer who thinks about experience — not just functionality.

If you're thinking about doing the same

I won't lie and say it's easy. It isn't. There are days when code feels like an alien language. There are moments when you watch tutorials from 22-year-olds explaining the basics and wonder if it's too late.

It's not.

  • You don't need to know everything before starting
  • Your previous experience is an asset, not a burden
  • The market needs developers who understand people
  • Starting late is better than never starting

"Fifteen years in retail taught me about people. Now I use that knowledge to build things people will use."

This is my story. It's still being written — and the next chapter is the most interesting one.
